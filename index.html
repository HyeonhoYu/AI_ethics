Core Principles of AI Ethics

Guidelines for ethical, transparent, and human-centered AI use in higher education.

Overview Table

PrincipleDescriptionFaculty Example

TransparencyMake clear when and how AI is usedInclude AI-use statements in syllabi
AccountabilityTake responsibility for AI-supported decisionsReview AI-generated grading before finalizing

Fairness

Source frameworks:

  • UNESCO (2021) — emphasizes “fairness, non-discrimination, and inclusiveness.”
  • IEEE (2022) — includes “justice, fairness, and equality” as guiding values.
  • ISTE (2023) — highlights “recognizing and mitigating bias.”

Adapted for faculty: “Use AI rubrics that promote linguistic inclusivity and fairness for all students.”

Transparency

Source frameworks:

  • UNESCO (2021) — calls for “transparency and explainability” of AI systems.
  • EDUCAUSE (2024) — encourages “disclosure of AI use in academic contexts.”
  • ISTE (2023) — advises teachers to “clearly communicate when AI tools are used.”

Adapted example: “Include AI-use statements in syllabi.”

Accountability

Source frameworks:

  • IEEE (2022) — central theme: “human responsibility for AI-driven outcomes.”
  • UNESCO (2021) — “AI actors must remain accountable for decisions and impacts.”
  • ISTE (2023) — “Educators remain ultimately responsible for ethical use of AI.”

Adapted example: “Review AI grading suggestions manually.”

Privacy

Source frameworks:

  • UNESCO (2021) — includes “protection of personal data and privacy.”
  • EDUCAUSE (2024) — “responsible data stewardship in AI adoption.”
  • FERPA and institutional policies — “confidentiality of student data.”

Adapted example: “Do not upload student essays to open AI tools.”

Human Oversight

Source frameworks:

  • UNESCO (2021) — insists AI must remain “under human oversight and control.”
  • OECD (2019) — “AI should augment human capabilities, not replace them.”
  • ISTE (2023) — “Keep humans in the loop for ethical and pedagogical judgment.”

Adapted example: “Use AI for feedback, not final evaluation.”
